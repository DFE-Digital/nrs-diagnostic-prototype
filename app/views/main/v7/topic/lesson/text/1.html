Article (part 1 of 3)

In the first part of this lesson, you will learn how to start a formal email. Knowing how to write a formal email is a key skill required in many industries and in everyday life. You may already write emails, so some of the information in this lesson may be familiar to you, but some of it might be new.

An email is a written message sent online to another person or a group of people.

Some common reasons to send an email are:

  • Writing an application (for a job)
  • Writing to a colleague at work (to request time off work or to arrange a meeting)
  • Writing a complaint and/ or asking for a refund (usually to a company who has failed to provide their services as you would expect)
  • Replying to an email from your child’s school

The email example we are going to work through in this lesson will be based on a template for a job application.

However, this lesson will be really useful no matter what type of email you are writing.

Read the following scenario:

You are currently a retail assistant and you want to improve your career prospects.Whilst browsing on a job centre website, you see a Sales Manager role advertised at Sony Electronics.

You need to write an email to apply for a job at Sony Electronics as a Sales Manager.

What are the key parts of an email?

  • The email address of the recipient spelt correctly
  • A subject line
  • A formal greeting
  • An opening line (clearly explaining your reason for writing)
  • Clear and relevant information
  • A formal closing and sign off

Recipient email address

In the ‘To:’ section of your email, you must write the email of the person you are sending it to. Remember, it is important to check the email is spelt correctly.

To: recruitment@sony.com

Subject line

  • Emails should include a formal subject line.
  • This should summarise the contents of the email.

Subject: Jane Smith - Application for Sales Manager role at Sony

Greeting

  • A formal email needs to use a greeting such as “Dear….” rather than “Hi” or “Hello”. This is because you may not have met the person you are writing to.
  • If you do not know the name of the person you are sending the application to (e.g. Mr Jones), you should use the title Sir/ Madam.

Opening line

  • The first sentence of your email should clearly introduce your purpose for writing.
  • In this example, it is clear this is an application for the role of Sales Manager at Sony.

Dear Sir/ Madam,

  • I am writing to apply for the position of Sales Manager at Sony Electronics.
